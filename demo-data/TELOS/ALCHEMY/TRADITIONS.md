# Traditions

> Initiatic lineages, learning traditions, and practices that shape my orientation.

---

## Open Source Culture

I grew up professionally in open source. The ethic of sharing knowledge, making code reviewable, and building on what others have made is foundational. It is not a political position — it is a way of thinking about knowledge and contribution.

What it asks of me: share what I build. Document what I learn. Credit what I use. Contribute upstream when I have something to offer.

---

## The Craft Tradition

The idea that work can be executed with care, that excellence is built through deliberate practice, and that the gap between adequate and excellent is worth crossing — this is what I mean by craft. It comes from reading about makers (Jiro, the Bauhaus, master typographers, systems programmers who wrote clean abstractions) and from the slow discipline of learning classical guitar bar by bar.

What it asks of me: go slower than feels productive. Redo the parts that are not right. Do not confuse completion with quality.

---

## Contemplative Practice

Meditation, silence, and regular reflection are not additions to the work — they are conditions for doing it well. Without a practice that slows the reactive mind, I cannot distinguish between a real insight and an anxious impulse to act.

What it asks of me: protect the morning. Sit before screen. Let the mind settle before the day asks things of it.

---

## The Local and the Global

Being based in Montreal, working in French and English, with clients in multiple industries — there is a tradition here of navigating plurality, of holding multiple frames simultaneously. This is an advantage, not a complication. The friction of translation creates understanding.

What it asks of me: do not flatten the complexity. Translate carefully. Respect what gets lost.
