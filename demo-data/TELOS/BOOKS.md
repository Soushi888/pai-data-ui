# Books

> Books that shaped my thinking. Annotated with what they changed.

---

## Design & Craft

**The Design of Everyday Things** — Don Norman
The foundational text on user-centered design. Taught me to blame the system, not the user. Still the most useful framework for evaluating interface decisions.

**Shape Up** — Ryan Singer (Basecamp)
Fixed time, variable scope. Changed how I scope and pitch client work. The concept of a "bet" instead of a spec is the most practical project management idea I have adopted.

**Thinking with Type** — Ellen Lupton
Typography as thought made visible. Reinforced my belief that the invisible decisions — spacing, weight, hierarchy — carry most of the meaning.

---

## Systems & Technology

**A Philosophy of Software Design** — John Ousterhout
Deep modules, shallow interfaces. Complexity management as the central challenge of software. Changed how I think about API surface area.

**Designing Data-Intensive Applications** — Martin Kleppmann
The clearest explanation of distributed systems tradeoffs I have read. Required reading before designing any system that handles real data.

**The Architecture of Open Source Applications** (various authors)
Case studies in how real systems were designed. Invaluable for understanding how design decisions compound over years.

---

## Business & Practice

**Company of One** — Paul Jarvis
A philosophy of intentional smallness. Validated my choice to stay a solo practitioner rather than grow into an agency.

**Getting Things Done** — David Allen
The capture habit. Not the whole system — just the discipline of getting things out of your head and into a trusted place. That part changed my daily life.

---

## Philosophy & Mind

**Antifragile** — Nassim Taleb
Systems that benefit from disorder. Changed how I think about redundancy, optionality, and the difference between robust and fragile dependencies.

**How to Think** — Alan Jacobs
A short, serious book about what makes thinking better or worse. Particularly useful on the topic of belonging to intellectual "tribes" and the cost to clarity.
