# Frames

> Mental lenses I use to interpret situations. Useful when I feel stuck or confused.

---

## The Five Whys

When something goes wrong (a project runs over, a client is unhappy, a system breaks), ask "why" five times before reaching for a solution. The first why reveals the symptom; the fifth approaches the root cause.

## Reversibility Test

Before any significant decision: is this reversible? Reversible decisions should be made fast and adjusted. Irreversible decisions (infrastructure, contracts, hires) warrant much more deliberation.

## The Stranger Test

Read the document, the code, the design as if you have never seen it before. Would a capable stranger understand it? Would they be confused? Would they trust it? This catches assumptions invisible to the author.

## Long Game vs. Short Game

Every decision has a short-game cost/benefit and a long-game cost/benefit. Most bad decisions optimize for the short game. When I feel pressure to rush, I ask: what does the long game version of this look like?

## Energy Accounting

Not all hours are equal. A focused morning hour is worth three interrupted afternoon hours. I budget attention like money — track where it goes and what it produces, and cut the spending that does not compound.

## The Minimum Useful Version

For any project or feature: what is the smallest version that actually solves the problem? Build that first. Resist the pull toward completeness before validation.

## Companion vs. Rescue

In collaborative and client work: I am a companion to the person's process, not a rescuer of it. Rescuing creates dependency and bypasses growth. Companionship means staying present and expert while the other person makes their own decisions.
