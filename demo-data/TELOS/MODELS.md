# Models

> Structural frameworks I rely on for decision-making and design.

---

## Shape Up (Basecamp)

Fixed time, variable scope. Assign a budget (a "bet") to a problem, not a spec. The team finds the solution within that budget. Prevents the bloat of requirements-driven development and the anxiety of open-ended scope. I use this for client Phase planning.

## Jobs to Be Done

People hire products to do a job. Understanding what job a user is trying to accomplish reveals requirements that feature lists never surface. Applied to client projects: understand the job before designing the solution.

## The PARA Method (Tiago Forte)

Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives — a four-category information architecture. Projects are active, time-bound. Areas are ongoing responsibilities. Resources are reference material. Archives are inactive. Simple and durable.

## Double Diamond (Design Council)

Diverge before converging, twice. First diamond: discover the right problem (diverge) → define it (converge). Second diamond: develop possible solutions (diverge) → deliver the chosen one (converge). Prevents solving the wrong problem with the right execution.

## The Lindy Effect

Technologies and practices that have survived for a long time are more likely to survive into the future than newer alternatives. Before adopting a new tool or framework, ask: will this be relevant in ten years? Plain text, SQLite, and HTTP have Lindy properties. Many JavaScript frameworks do not.

## Conway's Law

Systems are shaped by the communication structures of the organizations that build them. To change the architecture, change how the team communicates. When a system feels incoherent, look first at the team structure, not the code.
