# Wrong

> Things I believed that turned out to be false. An intellectual humility log.

---

## "More features = more value"

I spent two years building feature-rich client deliverables and wondering why adoption was low. The problem was scope, not effort. Users wanted fewer, better things. Minimal useful beats comprehensive underused every time.

**Updated belief:** Value comes from solving one problem exceptionally well, not ten problems adequately.

---

## "Clients want technical excellence"

I assumed clients cared about clean code, good architecture, and best practices. Most do not — at least not directly. They care about outcomes: does it work, is it fast, can my team maintain it?

**Updated belief:** Technical excellence serves the client indirectly, through reliability and maintainability. Lead with outcomes; let excellence show through, not be explained.

---

## "Saying yes builds relationships"

Taking every project offered, every favour asked, every scope expansion without pushback — I thought this would create goodwill. It created exhaustion and resentment.

**Updated belief:** Saying no clearly and early is more respectful than saying yes and delivering poorly.

---

## "Tools don't matter that much"

I used to believe good craftspeople work well regardless of tools. This is partially true and mostly false. Friction in tools compounds: bad tooling kills focus, slows iteration, and drains energy. Investing in the environment is investing in the work.

**Updated belief:** Tool selection and environment design are legitimate creative investments, not luxuries.

---

## "Distributed systems are for large teams"

I dismissed local-first and distributed architecture as over-engineering for my scale of work. It took building PAI Data UI to realize that sovereignty, resilience, and portability are valuable at any scale.

**Updated belief:** Architecture choices have values embedded in them. Choose tools that reflect the values you want to live with.
